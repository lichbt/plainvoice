## What Every Invoice Must Include

Think of an invoice like a receipt in reverse: instead of proving a payment was made, it proves a payment is owed. For it to do that job properly — and for your client to process it without asking follow-up questions — six things need to be on every invoice you send. [This detailed breakdown of what to include on an invoice](/blog/what-to-include-on-an-invoice/) goes through every must-have field with real examples if you want to go deeper after this walkthrough.

- ✅ **Your name or business name** — the person or entity the client is paying
- ✅ **Client's name and contact details** — so it reaches the right person in their organisation
- ✅ **Line items** — a specific description of what you provided, with quantity and price per unit
- ✅ **Totals** — a subtotal, any applicable tax, and the final amount owed
- ✅ **Payment terms** — a specific due date, not a vague phrase like "payment on receipt"
- ✅ **Payment method** — your bank details, PayPal address, or a payment link

Plainvoice has a dedicated field for every one of these. You will not accidentally leave one out.

## Step 2: Enter Your Name and Contact Details

Fill in the **"From"** section at the top of the form with your full name (or your business name if you have one), your email address, and optionally your phone number and mailing address.

Why it matters: your client needs to know who is billing them, and they need a way to reach you if they have a question before they pay. Your name and email are the two most important pieces of information on the entire invoice.

**Example:** Sam Okafor | sam@samokafor.com | +44 7700 900123

If you are a sole trader or freelancer, your own name is your business name — that is completely fine and perfectly legal. You do not need a registered company or a trading name to invoice a client professionally.

**Watch for:** Do not skip your email address. If a client has a billing question and cannot find your contact details on the invoice, they will often delay payment until they track you down through another channel.

## Step 3: Enter Your Client's Details

Fill in the **"Bill To"** section with your client's name or company name, their email address, and their business address if you have it.

Why it matters: many businesses — especially larger companies — require a correctly addressed invoice before their accounts team can process payment. Some internal systems will flag or delay an invoice that has incomplete client details. Getting this right at the start removes a very common source of delay.

**Example:** Horizon Media Ltd | accounts@horizonmedia.com | 45 Fleet Street, London, EC4Y 1BJ

**Watch for:** If you are unsure of the correct billing contact at a company, ask your client before you send the invoice — not after. "Who should I address the invoice to?" is a completely normal question that every client expects.

## Step 4: Add a Unique Invoice Number

Type a reference number into the **"Invoice #"** field. If this is your first invoice, INV-001 is a simple and clean starting point. Increase by one with every new invoice you create.

Why it matters: the invoice number is your reference ID — the shorthand that connects a payment to a specific project. When a client emails to confirm they have paid invoice 003, you know instantly which project and amount they mean. It also makes your records straightforward at tax time.

**Example:** INV-001 for your first invoice, INV-002 for your second. If you work with multiple regular clients, you can add a client code: HORIZ-001 for Horizon Media, GRNLF-001 for Greenleaf Studio.

**Watch for:** Never reuse an invoice number. Two invoices sharing the same number will cause real confusion if a payment dispute ever arises.

## Step 5: Set the Invoice Date

Enter today's date in the **"Invoice Date"** field. This is the date you are officially issuing the invoice — not the date you finished the work, just the date the invoice is going out the door.

Why it matters: the invoice date is the reference point for your payment due date. Every payment term — Net 14, Net 30, due in 7 days — is measured forward from this date. An incorrect invoice date shifts your entire payment timeline without you realising it.

**Example:** 22 June 2026

## Step 6: Add a Due Date — The Single Biggest Factor in Getting Paid on Time

Set a specific due date in the **"Due Date"** field. Choose a real calendar date — for example, 6 July 2026 — rather than writing "payment on receipt" or leaving the field blank.

Why it matters: an invoice without a due date has no deadline, and no deadline means no urgency. A concrete date creates a clear, professional expectation on both sides. Think of it like a train departure: a ticket with a specific time gets you on the platform; one that says "depart whenever" keeps you in the waiting room indefinitely. Freelancers who include a specific due date consistently get paid faster than those who leave payment terms vague.

If you are unsure what payment window to choose, [this complete guide to invoice payment terms for freelancers](/blog/invoice-payment-terms-freelancers/) explains Net 7, Net 14, and Net 30 — and which ones work best for different types of freelance work and client relationships.

**Example:** Invoice date 22 June 2026 + Net 14 terms = due date 6 July 2026.

**Watch for:** Do not write "ASAP" or "upon receipt." These phrases have no legal force and clients routinely treat them as optional.

## Step 7: Add Your Line Items

In the line items section, add one row for each distinct service or product you are billing for. Each row needs three things: a **description** of what you did, a **quantity**, and a **unit price**. Plainvoice calculates the row total and the overall total for you automatically.

Why it matters: a detailed line-item breakdown does two things at once. It removes any ambiguity about what the client is paying for, and it removes any excuse for the client to ask clarifying questions before approving the payment. Specificity is the freelancer's best protection against delayed payments.

## Step 8: Add Tax If Required

If you are required to charge tax on your services — VAT in the UK or EU, GST in Australia or Canada, or applicable sales tax in certain US states — enter your tax rate as a percentage in the tax field. Plainvoice calculates the tax amount and adds it to the total automatically.

Why it matters: charging the wrong tax rate, or forgetting to include tax you are obligated to collect, can create complications with your tax authority down the line. If you are not certain whether you need to charge tax as a freelancer in your country, [this plain-English guide to whether freelancers charge tax on invoices](/blog/do-freelancers-charge-tax-on-invoices/) covers the main scenarios for most English-speaking markets.

If you are just starting out and your annual income is below your country's tax registration threshold, you may not need to charge tax at all — the guide above will help you determine this in a few minutes.

**Example:** A UK-based freelancer registered for VAT adds 20% to the subtotal. A US-based freelancer billing for a service (not a physical product) to an out-of-state client often does not need to add sales tax at all.

## Step 9: Add Your Payment Method

In the **notes or payment details field**, tell your client exactly how to send you money. Include your full bank account details, PayPal email address, or a link to your preferred payment platform — whichever method you want them to use.

Why it matters: this is the step first-time invoicers most commonly skip, and it is one of the biggest single reasons invoices go unpaid for days after the due date. A client who genuinely wants to pay immediately still cannot do so if they do not know where to send the money. Make it effortless for them to pay you right now, in this moment, without having to ask you anything first.

**Watch for:** Do not assume your client already has your payment details from a previous email. Always include them directly on the invoice, every single time, so payment requires zero back-and-forth.

## Common Mistakes That Delay Your Payment

These are not hypothetical warnings — they are the specific, recurring errors freelancers make most often, and the concrete consequences each one creates:

- **Sending without a due date:** The invoice joins a "whenever" pile in the client's inbox with no deadline attached to it. Fix: always set a specific calendar date in the Due Date field, every time.
- **Leaving out payment instructions:** A client who wants to pay immediately but does not have your bank details will wait until they can ask you — adding days of delay through no one's fault. Fix: include your full payment method on every invoice, without exception.
- **Using vague line-item descriptions:** "Consulting work — May" gives a client a reason to pause and ask for clarification before approving payment. Fix: name each deliverable specifically, with dates, word counts, hours, or any other measurable detail that removes ambiguity.
- **Sending as a Word document or Google Doc link:** Editable formats look less professional and can be modified by accident. Fix: always send a PDF — Plainvoice creates one with a single click at no cost.
- **Missing your own contact details:** If a client has a billing question and cannot find your email on the invoice, they will delay payment until they track you down through another channel. Fix: put your email address in the From field on every invoice you send.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is it legal to send an invoice without accounting software?

Yes, completely. There is no legal requirement to use accounting software when invoicing a client. An invoice is simply a document that records what you provided and what you are owed. A PDF generated by a free online tool is just as legally valid as one produced by enterprise accounting software — what matters is that it contains the correct information, not which tool created it.

### Do I need to register as a business before I can invoice a client?

In most English-speaking countries, no. If you are working as a sole trader or independent freelancer, you can invoice clients using your own name. You do not need a company registration number or a formal business entity to receive payment for your work. Most countries only require registration once your income crosses a specific threshold — until then, your name and a valid invoice are all you need.

### Can I invoice as a sole trader or freelancer?

Yes — and this is the most common situation for people sending their first invoice. As a sole trader, you are the business. Your personal name is your business name. Put your full name in the From field, add your contact details, and your invoice is legitimate. You do not need a trading name, a logo, or a registered company number.

### What file format should I send my invoice in?

PDF is the standard, and the right choice in almost every situation. A PDF cannot be accidentally edited by the recipient, looks identical on every device and operating system, and is accepted by virtually every business accounting system. Plainvoice generates a clean PDF with a single click — no extra steps, no watermarks, no trial branding.

### Does my invoice need a logo or professional branding?

No. A logo can make an invoice look polished, but it is not required for the invoice to be valid or for your client to pay it. Many experienced freelancers send clean, text-based invoices their entire careers. Get the required fields right first — your name, client details, line items, totals, due date, and payment method. Branding is a nice bonus, not a requirement.

### What if I make a mistake on an invoice after I have already sent it?

Open Plainvoice, recreate the invoice with the corrected information, and email the updated version to your client. Use the same invoice number and include a short note explaining what changed — for example, "Please discard the previous version of INV-005 and use this corrected copy." Most clients appreciate the transparency and process the corrected invoice without any fuss.
