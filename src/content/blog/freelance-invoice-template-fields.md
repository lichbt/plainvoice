## The Scenario: A Web Designer's First Invoice

Throughout this walkthrough, we'll use a concrete example: **Jordan Lee**, a freelance web designer who just completed a 5-page website for **Oakwood Bakery**, a local small business. ## Every Invoice Field, One by One

### Your Contact Information

Place your full name or business name, email address, and phone number at the top of the invoice. This tells the client who to pay and who to call with questions. Don't skip the phone number — it provides a direct fallback for payment queries that shouldn't have to bounce through email chains.

**Jordan's invoice:** Jordan Lee · jordan@jordanlee.design · +1 (415) 555-0192

### Client Contact Information

Directly below your details: the client's full name, company name, and billing email. Always include the company name. Accounting departments file invoices by company, not by the name of the person you worked with. Sending to the wrong contact — or omitting the company entirely — is one of the most common reasons invoices get misfiled and forgotten.

**Jordan's invoice:** Oakwood Bakery · Attn: Sarah Chen (Owner) · billing@oakwoodbakery.com

### Invoice Number

A unique identifier assigned to this specific invoice — typically sequential: `INV-001`, `INV-002`, and so on. This is the most overlooked field on a first invoice and the easiest to fix. Most corporate accounting systems cannot process or route a payment for an unnumbered invoice. It either bounces back to you or sits in an inbox while someone means to follow up. Start at INV-001 on day one; never repeat or skip a number.

**Jordan's invoice:** INV-001

### Issue Date

The date the invoice is created and sent. It starts the payment clock for your terms. Use a specific calendar date — never "today," never blank. This date is what a client's accountant uses to verify whether an invoice is within terms or overdue.

**Jordan's invoice:** June 17, 2026

### Due Date

The single most important field on any invoice. A missing due date is functionally the same as no deadline — and no deadline creates no urgency. Include both the payment terms label *and* the resulting calendar date. Do not assume the client will count 14 or 30 days forward themselves. Make it impossible to misread.

**Jordan's invoice:** July 1, 2026 (Net 14)

### Payment Terms

The agreed payment window. Common options for freelancers:

- **Due on receipt** — immediate payment; appropriate for rush projects or established clients with a good track record
- **Net 7** — 7 days; ideal for small, fast-turnaround deliverables
- **Net 14** — the recommended freelancer default; balances healthy cash flow with client flexibility
- **Net 30** — standard for larger corporations but slow for independent contractors relying on steady income

State your late fee policy on the same line: "1.5%/month applied to balances unpaid after the due date" is the industry standard. Spelling it out on the invoice — not just buried in a contract PDF — eliminates all ambiguity and improves on-time payment rates.

**Jordan's invoice:** Net 14 · 1.5%/month late fee on overdue balances

### Line Items

The core of the invoice. Each line must include: a **description** of what was delivered, the **quantity** (hours worked or units delivered), the **unit rate**, and the **line total**. Vague entries like "website work" or "design services" are the single most common trigger for invoice disputes and clarification requests that delay payment by days. Be specific enough that someone who was not part of the project can understand exactly what was done.

| Description | Qty | Rate | Total |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Homepage design & development (custom layout, mobile-responsive) | 8 hrs | $95.00 | $760.00 |
| Interior pages × 4 (About, Menu, Contact, Gallery) | 12 hrs | $95.00 | $1,140.00 |
| SEO setup — meta tags, XML sitemap, Google Search Console | 2 hrs | $95.00 | $190.00 |
| Stock photography licence (5 images, Unsplash+) | 1 | $49.00 | $49.00 |

### Subtotal

The sum of all line items before tax or discounts. Label it clearly as its own line. Showing the subtotal before the grand total lets the client verify your arithmetic independently — transparent billing builds trust and dramatically reduces payment disputes.

**Jordan's invoice:** Subtotal: $2,139.00

### Tax

Include a dedicated tax line even if your rate is zero. "Tax: $0.00 (not applicable)" is cleaner than a missing field that prompts the client to ask. If you are required to collect sales tax, VAT, GST, or HST, state the rate and the amount explicitly. What you charge depends on your location, the client's location, and the type of service — consult a local accountant if you're unsure.

**Jordan's invoice:** Tax: $0.00 (digital services, CA non-taxable)

### Total Due

The final, unambiguous number the client owes. Bold it, enlarge it slightly, or put a border around it — whatever it takes to ensure it cannot be overlooked. The total should immediately follow the tax line so the client's eye travels cleanly from subtotal → tax → total due without needing to hunt.

**Jordan's invoice:** **Total Due: $2,139.00**

### Payment Methods

Specify exactly how you accept payment — don't leave this implicit. Common options: bank transfer (ACH/wire with your routing and account number), PayPal (your email), Wise, Venmo (business), or check. If you accept multiple methods, list all of them. Clients should not have to ask; every round-trip email is another day before the payment clears.

**Jordan's invoice:** Payment via bank transfer (routing: 121000248, account: 4571209863) or PayPal (jordan@jordanlee.design)

### Notes / Payment Terms Reminder

An optional but useful field for anything that doesn't belong elsewhere: a brief thank-you, a reference to the project contract, a reminder of your late-fee policy, or any agreed discount. Keep it to two or three lines maximum.

**Jordan's invoice:** Thank you for a great project, Sarah. Payment due July 1 — please reference INV-001 in your transfer. A 1.5%/month late fee applies to overdue balances.

## Common First-Invoice Mistakes

Even with a good template, first-time freelancers make the same handful of errors. Here's what to check before you hit send.

**Missing invoice number.** "There's no number on your invoice, so our system can't process it" is one of the most common replies a new freelancer gets from a corporate accounts payable team. Fix it before it happens: start at INV-001 and go up from there. Never skip or reuse a number.

**No specific due date.** Writing "Net 30" without writing "Due: July 17, 2026" forces the client to do the math themselves — and they won't always do it in your favor. Include both.

**Vague line-item descriptions.** "Design work — $3,000" gives a dispute a foothold. "Homepage design and development, custom layout, mobile-responsive (8 hrs @ $95/hr) — $760.00" does not. Be specific enough that someone outside the project can understand what was delivered.

**Sending to the wrong person.** Always confirm the billing contact before you send the first invoice. The colleague you worked with day-to-day is often not the person who processes payments. Ask once early; it saves multiple follow-up emails later.

**No payment method listed.** An invoice with a total and a due date but no instructions for how to pay creates unnecessary friction. Clients who have to ask how to pay will sometimes simply wait until it's convenient — which may be after your due date.

**Forgetting to attach it.** It sounds trivial, but it happens. Before sending, verify the PDF is actually attached to the email. A missing attachment usually delays payment by at least 24–48 hours while the exchange sorts itself out.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What should a freelance invoice include?

A freelance invoice should include your contact information, the client's company name and billing email, a unique invoice number, issue date, specific calendar due date, payment terms, itemized line items with descriptions and rates, subtotal, tax (or an explicit $0.00 line), total due, accepted payment methods, and optional notes. Missing even one of these — especially the invoice number or due date — can delay or block payment entirely.

### What payment terms should freelancers use?

Net 14 (payment due within 14 days) is the most freelancer-friendly default. It creates faster cash flow than Net 30 while still giving clients a reasonable window. Always include both the payment terms label and the specific calendar due date on every invoice — don't expect clients to count 14 or 30 days forward on their own.

### Do freelancers need to charge tax on invoices?

It depends on your location and service type. In the US, most digital and creative services are not subject to sales tax in most states, but rules vary by state. Freelancers in Canada, the UK, and the EU may be required to collect GST, HST, or VAT once annual revenue crosses a legal threshold. Consult a local accountant before adding or omitting tax from your invoices — getting it wrong in either direction creates legal risk.

### What is a freelance invoice number and why does it matter?

An invoice number is a unique identifier (e.g., INV-001) assigned to each invoice you send. It's required by most corporate accounting departments to process and file payments, and it's essential for your own bookkeeping. Without one, your invoice can be rejected or lost in a client's accounts payable system. Start at INV-001 and count up sequentially — never reuse or skip a number.

### How do I write a good line item description on a freelance invoice?

Be specific: describe exactly what was delivered, not just the service category. Instead of "Design services," write "Homepage design and development — custom layout, mobile-responsive (8 hrs @ $95/hr)." Include the deliverable type, scope, and hours or units. Vague descriptions are the single most common trigger for invoice disputes and requests for clarification that stall payment.

### What's the difference between a freelance invoice and a receipt?

An invoice is a request for payment sent before money is received. A receipt is a confirmation of payment issued after the transaction is complete. As a freelancer, you send the invoice first; after payment is received, you may optionally issue a receipt. Both documents should reference the same invoice number for clean record-keeping and easy reconciliation.

### Can I use a Word or Google Docs template for freelance invoices?

Yes — a Word or Google Docs template works well for low-volume freelancers sending fewer than four or five invoices per month. The limitation is purely manual: you calculate totals yourself, track overdue invoices yourself, and send follow-up emails by hand. Once your client volume grows beyond that threshold, dedicated invoicing software saves more time than it costs.
